MGA

Muaaz G. Awan, Ph.D.

Senior Systems Software Engineer at NVIDIA

I'm a high-performance computing engineer who turns cutting-edge hardware into faster science. At NVIDIA, I develop and optimize GPU-accelerated genomics pipelines for the company's accelerated computing platform.

My journey began with a Ph.D. in Computer Science, where I designed high-performance strategies to tame the massive datasets produced by mass spectrometry–based proteomics. I then joined Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory as a postdoctoral fellow and went on to spend several years there as an HPC expert — supporting some of the world's leading-class supercomputers and building and optimizing the scientific applications that run on them.

What I Do

I work at the intersection of modern supercomputing hardware and real scientific workloads — squeezing performance out of GPUs and getting demanding research applications to run at scale.

GPU Acceleration

Designing and optimizing GPU kernels and libraries (CUDA, HIP, SYCL) that move production scientific workflows onto accelerated hardware — from sequence alignment to genome assembly.

Performance Engineering

Profiling, modeling, and tuning applications for exascale-era systems, and co-developing performance models like the instruction roofline to reason about where time really goes.

Computational Bioinformatics

Building large-scale genomics and proteomics pipelines — metagenome assembly, protein homology search, and mass-spectrometry data reduction — that turn big biological data into insight.

Leadership-Class Systems

Benchmarking, acceptance, and readiness work for some of the world's fastest supercomputers, ensuring they deliver sustained performance for science.

Experience

NERSC — Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory

2019 – 2026

Application Performance Specialist & Postdoctoral Fellow

Spent several years as an HPC expert supporting NERSC's leadership-class supercomputers and the scientists who rely on them. A few highlights:

  • Built GPU-optimized libraries (including ADEPT and local-assembly kernels) that enabled GPU offloading of production scientific workflows on Perlmutter, Frontier, and Aurora.
  • Developed and optimized a large-scale metagenome assembler for exascale-era hardware as part of the Exascale Computing Project.
  • Co-developed the instruction roofline model and led performance-portability studies across multiple vendor GPUs.
  • Co-led the system benchmarking and acceptance teams for the NERSC-10 procurement.

Earlier Research

2013 – 2019

Before Berkeley, I completed a Ph.D. in Computer Science at Western Michigan University (advised by Dr. Fahad Saeed), where my dissertation focused on high-performance reductive strategies for big data from LC-MS/MS proteomics. Along the way I interned with the Integrative Omics group at Pacific Northwest National Laboratory and worked on GPU-accelerated data-mining and disease-surveillance systems.
